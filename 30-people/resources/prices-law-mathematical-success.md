## The Core Formula

**Price's Law (1963):** Discovered by physicist Derek J. de Solla Price

> "The square root of the number of people in a domain does 50% of the work."

**Mathematical notation:** √n people = 50% of output

---

## The Pattern is Universal

### Examples Across Domains

**Organizations:**
- 100-person company → 10 employees drive half the output
- 10,000 scientists → 100 generate half the research
- 25-person team → 5 individuals carry operations

**Markets:**
- **Spotify:** 3,300 artists (out of 11 million) generate 50% of all streams
- **US Economy:** 5,500 businesses (of millions) produce 50% of total output

**Your work:**
- 40 hours per week → ~6 hours are high-leverage
- 730 pieces of content → ~17 pieces (2.3%) drove majority of growth

---

## Why This Happens

**It's not unfairness—it's mathematical inevitability.**

Real competence requires **all four** compounding together:
1. **Skill**
2. **Consistency**
3. **Opportunity**
4. **Luck**

**Most people have 1-2 of these.**
**The √n group has all 4.**

This is why "working hard" isn't enough. You need the right combination of factors compounding over time.

## Practical Applications

### 1. Content Creation Strategy

**Volume → Track → Pattern → Double Down**

1. **Produce volume initially** (50-100 pieces minimum)
2. **Track metrics rigorously** (what performs, what doesn't)
3. **Identify patterns** (which 2-3% drive results)
4. **Double down ruthlessly** on what works

**Example:** Author produced 730+ outputs, only ~2.3% drove majority growth.

**Lesson:** You can't predict winners upfront. You have to create volume, then let the data show you.

---

### 2. Skills Development

**Don't be "well-rounded" — be strategically spiky.**

**Wrong approach:** Top 10% at 10 things
**Right approach:** Top 1% combination of 2-3 multiplier skills

**Example combinations:**
- Writing × Distribution × Product sense
- Sales × Technical understanding × Industry relationships
- Design × Psychology × Analytics

**The math:** Being top 10% at 3 things = top 0.1% combination (10% × 10% × 10% = 0.1%)

**This is your √n in skills.**

---

### 3. Time Management

**Of 40 weekly hours, ~6 are high-leverage.**

**The question isn't:** "How do I be productive for 40 hours?"
**The question is:** "Which 6 hours move the needle, and how do I protect them?"

**High-leverage activities:**
- Client calls that close deals
- Product decisions that 10x value
- Content that reaches 100K people
- Strategic conversations that unlock new markets

**Low-leverage activities:**
- Email (mostly)
- Internal meetings (mostly)
- "Busy work" that feels productive but doesn't compound

**Optimize for the 6, not the 40.**

## The Explore-Exploit Paradox

**Success requires two phases:**

### Phase 1: EXPLORE (Find your √n)
- Try many things
- Track what works
- Look for patterns
- Be open to surprise

### Phase 2: EXPLOIT (Double down on winners)
- Go all-in on what's working
- Cut everything else ruthlessly
- Compound the advantage
- Protect the high-leverage hours

**Most people fail by:**
1. **Exploring endlessly** (never committing, always "trying new things")
2. **Exploiting prematurely** (specializing before finding what works)

**Balance is essential.**

You need 50-100 reps to identify the pattern. Then you need the discipline to cut the 97% that doesn't work.

## How to Apply This Week-by-Week

### Sunday Review: Find Your √n

**Step 1 - Time Allocation:**
- Which 6 hours this week were high-leverage?
- Which 34 hours were necessary but not √n?
- What can you delegate/delete/defer?

**Step 3 - Lessons Learned:**
- Where did you get 10x ROI on time?
- Which activities compounded?
- What surprised you?

**Step 4 - The One Decision:**
- What's the single √n activity for next week?
- Everything else supports this or gets cut

### Monday Execution: Protect the √n

**Time blocking:**
- √n work gets morning (highest energy)
- Low-leverage gets afternoon or delegation
- Buffer 25% for unexpected (don't pack to 100%)

## Real-World Example: Milano Bonifica

**The volume approach:**
- 3 contractor emails (explore)
- Track which ones respond
- First positive call = pattern identified
- Double down ruthlessly on that relationship

**Wrong approach:**
- Email 1 contractor perfectly
- Wait for response
- If no response, give up

**Right approach (Price's Law):**
- Email all 3 simultaneously
- Track response rate
- The one who responds is your √n
- Go all-in on that partnership

**This is explore → exploit.**

## Connecting to Justin Welsh's "Freedom Through Constraints"

**Price's Law + Chosen Constraints = Leverage**

**Justin Welsh:** Choose your constraints intentionally (rigid schedule = freedom)

**Price's Law:** Within those constraints, find the √n activities (6 high-leverage hours)

**Combined:**
1. **Structure your week** (Sunday Review → Monday time blocks)
2. **Identify your √n** (which 6 hours matter most?)
3. **Protect those hours** (rigid constraints enable focus)
4. **Cut everything else** (explore-exploit: find winners, double down)

**Result:** You're not "busy." You're focused on the √n that drives 50% of results.

## When to Re-Read This

- When spreading yourself too thin (trying to do everything)
- When feeling guilty about "not working hard enough" (volume ≠ leverage)
- When starting a new project (remember: explore first, exploit after pattern emerges)
- When Sunday Review shows misalignment (40 hours worked, but not on the √n)
- When comparing yourself to people who "do more" (they're probably not on their √n either)

**Remember:** √100 = 10. You don't need to do 100 things. You need to find the 10 that matter, then go all-in.
