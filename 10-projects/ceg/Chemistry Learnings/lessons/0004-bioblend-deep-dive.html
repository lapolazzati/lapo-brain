🦠 Lesson 0004: BioBlend Deep Dive

The biological scalpel: how bacteria strip chlorines one at a time, and why stopping halfway is worse than not starting

You Are Feeding a Garden, Not Building a Factory

ISCO is the chemical sledgehammer — you inject persulfate and radicals break bonds in hours. BioBlend is the opposite: you add food, wait, and let bacteria do the work over months. The bacteria are already there. They've been eating trace organics in the groundwater for millennia. You're not importing them. You're just giving them a clear menu and a comfortable dining room.

The headline: one Cl atom leaves, one H atom arrives. Repeat until the molecule is harmless.

Step 1: The Bacteria Are Already There

Underground, in every aquifer on Earth, there are bacteria. They don't have a fancy brand name. They're Dehalococcoides, Dehalobacter, Geobacter — and dozens of others. They live on whatever organic carbon drifts past. Usually that's humic acids, plant debris, tiny fragments of natural molecules. It's a slow life, but it's their life.

When you spill TCE into that aquifer, something interesting happens: the bacteria can use it as an electron acceptor. Not all of them. Not quickly. But under the right conditions — no oxygen, plenty of food (electron donor) — the right strains wake up and start stripping chlorines off the TCE molecule.

This is biostimulation — CEG BioBlend's approach. You're stimulating the native microbial community. You're not adding foreign bacteria (that's bioaugmentation, which CEG rarely uses because it introduces regulatory complexity and questionable performance in Italian aquifers).

Step 2: Reductive Dechlorination — One Cl at a Time

Reductive dechlorination is a stepwise process. Each step removes one chlorine atom and replaces it with hydrogen. The molecule gets lighter, less toxic, more biodegradable — but some of the intermediate steps are dangerous.

Each arrow is one reductive dechlorination step. The chlorine leaves as Cl⁻ (chloride ion — harmless). A hydrogen takes its place. The molecule shrinks. Eventually you have ethene: a simple two-carbon gas with no chlorine at all. Ethene is harmless. It's even produced naturally by ripening fruit.

Why VC is the nightmare: Vinyl chloride is a known human carcinogen. Worse than TCE in some respects. If your bioremediation stalls at VC — if the bacteria run out of food, if conditions change, if the wrong strain dominates — you've traded one problem for a worse one.

This is why monitoring matters. You don't celebrate when TCE drops. You celebrate when ethene appears. If you see VC accumulating without ethene, the process is stalled and you need to intervene.

Step 3: What the Food Actually Is — Electron Donors

Bacteria need two things to do reductive dechlorination: (1) an electron acceptor (the TCE molecule — it accepts electrons as chlorines are stripped off), and (2) an electron donor (food — the molecule that provides electrons and hydrogen).

Without the donor, the bacteria have no energy. The reaction stops. With too little donor, the process stalls at VC. With the right donor in the right amount, the bacteria thrive and push TCE all the way to ethene.

Lactate
CH₃CH(OH)COO⁻
The precision tool. Ferments to acetate, propionate, and hydrogen — all usable by dechlorinators. Fast-acting, well-studied. Common in CEG BioBlend formulations for high-priority source zones.
Molasses
Mix of sugars
The cheap bulk option. Sucrose and other sugars ferment to organic acids and hydrogen. Slower release than lactate but lower cost. Good for large plumes where cost per kg matters and timeline allows.
Emulsified Vegetable Oil
Long-chain fatty acids
The slow drip. Hydrogen is released gradually over months as the oil biodegrades. Best for long-term plume control where you want sustained low-level activity without repeat injections.

CEG's choice depends on the project: lactate for fast response in source zones with high TCE, molasses for large dilute plumes where cost dominates, emulsified oils for long-term passive barriers.

Step 4: Monitoring — When Do You Know It's Working?

This is the ARPA question that separates professionals from pretenders. How do you prove the bacteria are doing their job?

You monitor three things:

What to Measure What It Tells You Target Reading Red Flag
TCE concentration Is the parent contaminant dropping? Declining trend over 3–6 months Flat or rising = process not active
cis-1,2-DCE + VC Are intermediates forming and then leaving? Brief spike then decline VC accumulation = stall at danger zone
Ethene / Ethane Did dechlorination go to completion? Appearance = success Never appears = incomplete process
ORP Is the zone anaerobic enough? < −100 mV > +50 mV = too oxidizing, wrong bacteria
pH Is fermentation producing excess acid? 6.0 – 8.5 < 5.5 = acid inhibition of bacteria
Chloride (Cl⁻) Is chlorine actually being released? Rising trend Flat = no dechlorination occurring

The magic signal: ethene and ethane in the monitoring well. If you see ethene, the bacteria have pushed through VC. The job is essentially done. Ethane (C₂H₆) is a further reduction product — even more reduced, even more harmless. Its presence is extra confirmation.

Step 5: BioBlend vs ISCO — When to Use Which

Factor ISCO (Chemical) BioBlend (Biological)
Speed Days to weeks Months to 1–2 years
Cost per kg treated Higher (chemicals, energy) Lower (food + waiting)
Best for High-concentration source zones, tight timelines Large dilute plumes, long-term polishing
Byproducts Cl⁻, SO₄²⁻, CO₂, H₂O Cl⁻, ethene/ethane, CO₂, H₂O
Intermediate risk Low (goes to mineralization directly) High (VC is a dangerous stop)
Redox needed Any (works in aerobic or anaerobic) Strictly anaerobic (reducing)
ARPA preference Fast results, well-documented chemistry Lower cost, "natural" process appeal

Many CEG projects use both: ISCO for the hot source zone where concentrations are highest, BioBlend for the downgradient plume where concentrations have diluted and time allows biological treatment. This is called a treatment train — the right tool for each zone.

Things That Can Go Wrong

  • Oxygen intrusion: Even small amounts of dissolved oxygen inhibit dechlorinating bacteria. A nearby pumping well, seasonal water table changes, or a leaky monitoring well can ruin the anaerobic zone. Solution: seal monitoring wells, protect the injection area from hydraulic interference.
  • pH crash: Fermentation of electron donors produces organic acids. Too much acid = pH drops below 5.5 = bacteria stop working. Solution: buffer the injection (add bicarbonate) or use slower-release donors like oils instead of lactate.
  • Stalling at VC: The most common failure mode. TCE drops, DCE appears, VC accumulates — and stays there. Why? Insufficient electron donor, wrong bacterial community, or competing electron acceptors (sulfate, nitrate) stealing resources. Solution: increase donor dose, check ORP, confirm absence of competing terminal electron acceptors.
  • Sulfate competition: If the aquifer has high sulfate (SO₄²⁻), sulfate-reducing bacteria outcompete dechlorinators for the electron donor. You feed the wrong bacteria. TCE sits untouched. Solution: measure sulfate before design; if high, ISCO may be better than BioBlend.
  • Insufficient contact time: Unlike ISCO where radicals attack in microseconds, bacteria need weeks to establish biomass. One injection may not be enough. Solution: design for re-injection or slow-release formulation.

The ARPA Conversation You'll Actually Have

🇮🇹 Scenario: ARPA questions biological treatment

ARPA: "You're proposing bioremediation for this plume. How do you know the bacteria will actually dechlorinate the TCE, and how do you prevent the formation of vinyl chloride?"

You: "We use biostimulation — we inject electron donors like lactate or emulsified vegetable oil to feed the native dechlorinating bacteria already present in the aquifer. These bacteria perform reductive dechlorination: each step removes one chlorine atom from TCE, producing cis-1,2-DCE, then vinyl chloride, then finally ethene — a harmless gas. The key is maintaining anaerobic conditions and ensuring sufficient donor supply to push the reaction past the VC stage. We monitor for ethene as our success indicator — not just TCE decline. If we see VC accumulating without ethene, that's our signal to increase donor dosage or adjust pH. We never declare success until ethene appears in the monitoring wells."

ARPA: "And the timeline? ISCO would be faster."

You: "Correct — ISCO is faster, and we use it for the high-concentration source zone. But for this downgradient plume at 50–200 micrograms per liter, BioBlend is more cost-effective over 12–18 months, with a lower chemical footprint. Our monitoring protocol gives us monthly data on TCE, VC, ethene, and ORP. If we see the process stall, we can switch to ISCO at any point — but our pilot data suggests the native bacteria are already active."

✓ Mechanism explained step by step. VC danger acknowledged and addressed. Monitoring protocol named. Hybrid approach justified. ARPA has confidence you know the failure modes.

Interactive Check

Self-test

Q1: What is the key difference between biostimulation and bioaugmentation?

✅ Correct. CEG BioBlend is biostimulation — you feed the bacteria already in the ground. Bioaugmentation imports microbes, which raises regulatory questions and has mixed field performance.
❌ Neither approach is defined by which gas it uses. Both operate under anaerobic conditions.
❌ Speed depends on site conditions, not the approach category. Biostimulation is the standard CEG approach regardless of speed.

Q2: Why is vinyl chloride (VC) more dangerous than TCE in the context of bioremediation?

✅ Correct. VC is a carcinogen. The dechlorination pathway TCE → DCE → VC → ethene can stall at VC if electron donors run out, pH crashes, or oxygen intrudes. A stalled BioBlend process that leaves VC behind is worse than no treatment at all.
❌ Solubility differences exist but are not the primary concern. The danger is toxicity + process stall.
❌ Both TCE and VC are dense enough to act as DNAPLs. Density is not what makes VC dangerous in this context.

Q3: Which monitoring parameter proves that reductive dechlorination went to completion?

✅ Correct. TCE decline alone doesn't prove completion — the process could have stalled at VC. Ethene (and ethane) is the only signal that the bacteria pushed through all four steps. ORP should be negative; rising ORP means the zone is becoming aerobic, which stops dechlorination entirely.
❌ TCE could be disappearing via dilution or sorption, not dechlorination. You need the product (ethene) to prove the pathway.
❌ Rising ORP is bad — it means oxygen is entering and killing the anaerobic bacteria. BioBlend needs ORP below −100 mV.
