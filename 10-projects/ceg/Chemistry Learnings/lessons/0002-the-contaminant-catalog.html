🧪 Lesson 0002: The Contaminant Catalog

Every player on the field — and which CEG tech handles each one

The Site Report Is a Casting Call

Every contaminated site has a roster. Some contaminants are the leads (high concentration, high toxicity). Some are extras (below threshold, negligible risk). Your job when you pick up a site report is to read the credits — name every player, know their signature move, and match it to CEG's toolkit before ARPA asks you.

"What matters, at what bond, and which technology?" That's the entire lesson.

Speed品类: BTEX — The Petroleum Gang

BTEX stands for benzene, toluene, ethylbenzene, xylene. They arrive together in gasoline, diesel, and fuel spills. If you see one, the others probably followed in the same plume. They share a family trait: an aromatic ring. Break the ring, you break the threat.

⛽ Benzene — Most toxic
Ring = target
CSC: 1 μg/L · LNAPL (floats)
🛢️ Toluene / Ethylbenzene / Xylene — Same family
Toluene CSC: 70 μg/L · Xylene: 50 μg/L
All LNAPLs · all ring-attackable
Lego brick: Aromatic ring
CEG tech: ISCO (ring-opening) + BioBlend (biodegradation)
Plume: LNAPL — floats on water table

Benzene is the king of this crew — it has the lowest CSC (1 μg/L) and is the most carcinogenic. Toluene is less toxic, xylene (three isomers) sits in the middle. When you see BTEX, start with the ring. ISCO ring-opens it with persulfate radicals; BioBlend lets bacteria chew the opened fragments down to CO₂ and H₂O.

The Chlorinated Crew — Sinks, Doesn't Float

Chlorinated solvents are DNAPLs (Dense Non-Aqueous Phase Liquids). They sink through the aquifer and pool on low-permeability layers at the bottom. This is why they're harder to find and harder to treat than petroleum. The Lego brick is the C–Cl bond on every one of these.

TCE = three chlorines (trichloro), PCE = four chlorines (tetrachloro, also called perchloroethylene). More chlorines = harder to break = more persulfate needed or slower bioremediation. PCE takes longer in BioBlend because there are four Cl atoms to strip off. Both are classic ISCO targets.

PAHs — The Coal Tar Monsters

Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons: multiple benzene rings fused together. Naphthalene = 2 rings. Benzo(a)pyrene = 5 rings. The bigger they are, the more persistent and carcinogenic they become.

🌑 Naphthalene — 2 rings
CSC: 2 μg/L soil · 0.02 μg/L water
Smaller = more treatable
🦠 Benzo(a)pyrene — 5 rings · carcinogen
CSC: 0.1 μg/L soil · extremely low in water
Large PAHs resist both ISCO and BioBlend
Small PAHs (2–3 rings): Naphthalene, anthracene — ISCO works
Large PAHs (4+ rings): BioBlend for polishing, or ex-situ incineration

Heavy Metals: A Different Game Entirely

Key principle: You cannot "destroy" a metal. It's an element. It stays on the periodic table forever.

With organic contaminants, you break bonds and the molecule ceases to exist. With metals, you can't break the atom. You can only change its form — oxidation state, solubility, mobility — so it stops hurting people.

Cr-VI (chromate) is toxic, mobile, and carcinogenic. Cr-III is low-toxicity and precipitates as chromium hydroxide, which stays put. The same total chromium atoms are still there — you've just changed their behavior. CSC for Cr-VI in groundwater: 5 μg/L.

Lead: Phosphate stabilization → insoluble pyromorphite
Cadmium: Precipitation / immobilization
Mercury / Arsenic: Specialized — often ex-situ

The ARPA Conversation You'll Actually Have

🇮🇹 Scenario: Milano site — Bovisasca

ARPA: "The site characterization found benzene at 120 μg/L, TCE at 45 μg/L, and naphthalene at 0.8 μg/L in groundwater. What are you proposing?"

You (now): "Three distinct contaminant classes, three different mechanisms. For the benzene — it's above CSC — that's an aromatic ring target. ISCO ring-opening with persulfate radicals will cleave the ring, making linear fragments the native bacteria can finish. TCE is also above its 1.5 μg/L CSC and has three C–Cl bonds — our primary approach is Advanced Oxidation. Naphthalene at 0.8 is above 0.02, so it matters too — ISCO handles two-ring PAHs well. We'll profile redox conditions first: if the deeper zone is anaerobic, BioBlend can handle residual concentrations after ISCO. The treatment train is ISCO first for concentration, BioBlend for polishing."

✓ Every contaminant named. Every threshold cited. Every technology matched to a bond or mechanism. No hand-waving.

Interactive Check

Self-test

Q1: A site report shows PCE at 8 μg/L. Which two facts must you know before choosing the technology?

✅ Correct. PCE is always a DNAPL — it sinks — so you need to know where it pooled. Redox tells you whether ISCO or BioBlend is the primary choice. Both are always the first two facts.
❌ Porosity matters for injection radius calculations, but not for technology choice. Price matters for the bid, not for mechanism.
❌ Source identification helps but doesn't drive chemistry. You already know PCE = dry cleaner solvent. Chemistry doesn't care where the pipe leaked.

Q2: You see benzo(a)pyrene at 0.5 μg/L in soil. Which statement is correct?

✅ Correct. BaP CSC is 0.1 μg/L in soil — 0.5 is 5× over. Large PAHs are recalcitrant. ISCO may partially oxidize but full degradation of 5-ring PAHs often requires ex-situ treatment or long-term BioBlend polishing.
❌ BaP CSC is 0.1 μg/L — not 1. This is a common mix-up with benzene's 1 μg/L threshold.
❌ Five fused aromatic rings don't "single-pass" open. Partial oxidation can create even more reactive intermediates. This is why large PAHs need careful treatment design.

Q3: A colleague says "We can just ISCO everything — metals, PFAS, solvents, PAHs." What's wrong with this?

✅ Correct. ISCO breaks covalent bonds on organic molecules. Metals are elements — no bonds to break, only oxidation states to change. PFAS has C–F bonds (~485 kJ/mol) that persulfate radicals can't reliably break. Technology matching matters.
❌ ISCO is powerful but not magic. "One technology for all" is how you lose credibility in front of ARPA.
❌ ISCO absolutely works on smaller PAHs (2–3 rings) and aromatic hydrocarbons. The limitation is large PAHs and non-organics.

What you can now say with confidence

  • "Benzene, toluene, xylene — all share an aromatic ring. ISCO ring-opens it; BioBlend degrades the fragments."
  • "TCE and PCE are DNAPLs — they sink. More chlorines means more C–Cl bonds to break. ISCO is primary; BioBlend works if anaerobic."
  • "Small PAHs (naphthalene): ISCO handles them. Large PAHs (benzo[a]pyrene): much harder — consider ex-situ or extended BioBlend."
  • "Metals aren't destroyed — they're transformed. Cr-VI → Cr-III by reduction. Lead → pyromorphite by stabilization."
  • "I can read a contaminant list, cite the CSC thresholds, and tell you the Lego brick and the CEG technology for each one."
