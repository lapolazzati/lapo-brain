⚗️ Lesson 0005: Heavy Metals

Different beast, different rules: why you can't "destroy" a metal, and how CEG makes it harmless anyway

You Can't Burn a Metal Into Nothing

With TCE, you can break C-Cl bonds and turn the molecule into CO₂ and water — the contaminant is destroyed. But a metal is an element. Chromium doesn't become "not chromium." Lead doesn't evaporate into thin air. You can't break a metal atom into smaller, harmless pieces.

So what's the strategy? You change its shape, its charge, or where it lives — making it toxic but stuck, or mobile but safe. This lesson is about the transformation game.

Step 1: The Fundamental Difference

Organic contaminants (TCE, benzene, PCE) are made of carbon, hydrogen, and a few other atoms arranged in molecules. Those molecules can be dismantled. Break the right bonds and the toxic structure is gone.

Metals are different. Chromium (Cr) is element 24 on the periodic table. It has 24 protons. You can't remove protons with chemistry — that takes a nuclear reactor. The same is true for lead (Pb, 82), cadmium (Cd, 48), mercury (Hg, 80), and arsenic (As, 33).

The goal isn't elimination — it's immobilization or detoxification. You make the metal stop moving through groundwater. You make it stop being absorbed by human cells. You reduce its bioavailability.

Step 2: Chromium — The Two Faces of Cr

Chromium is the perfect teaching case because it shows exactly why oxidation state matters. Same element, two very different behaviors.

Cr⁶⁺ (Cr-VI)
→
Reduction + electrons
From ISCO byproducts or bacteria
→
Cr³⁺ (Cr-III)
Cr-VI: carcinogenic, mobile, water-soluble | Cr-III: low toxicity, precipitates as Cr(OH)₃

Cr-VI (hexavalent chromium) is what Erin Brockovich made famous. It's what caused the contamination in Hinkley, California. In Italy, it's common near tanneries, electroplating shops, and metal finishing facilities. It dissolves easily in water and moves with groundwater. It's carcinogenic when inhaled or ingested.

Cr-III (trivalent chromium) is what your body actually uses in trace amounts for insulin metabolism. It's much less toxic. And critically — Cr-III is insoluble in neutral-to-alkaline water. It precipitates out as chromium hydroxide, Cr(OH)₃, and sticks to soil particles. Immobile. Safe.

Property Cr-VI Cr-III
Toxicity High — carcinogen Low — essential trace element
Solubility Very soluble Insoluble (precipitates)
Mobility High — travels with groundwater Low — sticks to soil
How to make Oxidation of Cr-III Reduction of Cr-VI
CSC in groundwater 5 μg/L (Italy) 50 μg/L (Italy)

The remediation strategy: Reduce Cr-VI to Cr-III. You can do this chemically (ISCO byproducts like ferrous iron, Fe²⁺, are excellent reducing agents) or biologically (certain bacteria reduce Cr-VI as part of their metabolism). Once reduced, Cr-III precipitates naturally and stays put.

The ARPA question: "If the metal is still in the ground, how do you know it won't turn back into Cr-VI?"

Your answer: "Cr-III is stable in the reducing conditions we create. As long as the zone stays anaerobic, Cr-III remains insoluble. We monitor ORP and pH long-term to confirm stability. If conditions shift, we can re-treat."

Step 3: Lead — The Phosphate Trick

Lead is a classic "stabilize in place" metal. You don't remove it. You lock it.

The chemistry is elegant: phosphate + lead → pyromorphite. Pyromorphite is a lead phosphate mineral. It's one of the most stable lead compounds known. Once formed, lead in pyromorphite doesn't dissolve in water, doesn't leach, and doesn't get absorbed by plants or human digestive systems.

Pb²⁺ in soil
mobile, toxic, bioavailable
→
Add phosphate
PO₄³⁻ (apatite, rock phosphate)
→
Pb₅(PO₄)₃Cl
pyromorphite — immobile, stable

This is called in situ stabilization or phytostabilization (if plants are involved). The lead is still there — you haven't dug it up, you haven't shipped it anywhere — but it's chemically imprisoned. ARPA understands this. It's accepted practice.

CEG relevance: In areas near old gas stations, shooting ranges, or battery recycling facilities in Milano, lead is common. CEG doesn't have a specific "lead technology" — but CEG needs to know when to recommend stabilization (usually with phosphate amendments) rather than excavation.

Step 5: CSC Thresholds for Metals

These are the numbers ARPA cares about. Memorize the big ones.

Metal Form Soil CSC (mg/kg) Groundwater CSC (μg/L) Health driver
Chromium Cr-VI 2 (residential) 5 Carcinogen
Chromium Cr-III 150 50 Low toxicity
Lead Pb total 100 10 Neurotoxin (children)
Cadmium Cd total 2 (residential) 5 Kidney damage
Mercury Hg total 1 1 Neurotoxin
Arsenic As total 20 (residential) 10 Carcinogen

Key observation: The Cr-VI soil CSC is 2 mg/kg. The Cr-III CSC is 150 mg/kg. That's a 75× difference. If you can prove site chromium is Cr-III (and that it won't convert back), the cleanup target gets dramatically easier. This is where reduction pays off — not just chemically, but regulatorily.

Step 6: CEG's Position on Metals

🎯 The Real Conversation with ARPA

ARPA: "Can your ISCO or BioBlend remove heavy metals?"
You: "No — metals can't be destroyed. But we have two strategies that work. First, the reducing conditions in BioBlend zones convert Cr-VI to Cr-III, which precipitates and stays put. Second, we can amend soils with phosphate to lock lead in place as pyromorphite. Both are standard, accepted approaches."
ARPA: "And if mercury is present?"
You: "Mercury is the exception — we typically recommend excavation and off-site treatment for elemental or methylmercury. It's volatile and methylates in situ, so leaving it in place is rarely acceptable."

CEG doesn't need a 'metal technology' in the same way it has ISCO and BioBlend. What CEG needs is knowledge: knowing when a metal requires excavation vs. stabilization vs. reduction; knowing which ARPA thresholds apply; knowing how to answer the "can you destroy metals?" question honestly and competently.
