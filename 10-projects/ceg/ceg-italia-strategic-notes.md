## Market context

Market context

Italy has roughly 18,000 contaminated sites nationally. As of January 1, 2024, MOSAICO (Italy's official national contaminated sites database, maintained by ISPRA) records **38,556 bonifica procedures: 16,365 ongoing and 22,191 concluded**. Almost one in two Italian municipalities is affected by at least one bonifica procedure. PFAS demand is especially acute: the Veneto case (Miteni/Trissino) alone covers 380 km² and 300,000 people exposed, with a court-ordered bonifica plan due mid-2025.

These aren't estimated figures. They're government-tracked, publicly searchable by region, contaminant type, and regulatory stage. The pipeline is documented.

Most of those 16,000 open procedures have been sitting for years. Italy's default technology — scavo e smaltimento — is slow, expensive, and produces contaminated waste that has to go somewhere. The system is clogged because the dominant approach doesn't scale. That's the opening.

One important procedural note: under Italian law (D.Lgs. 152/2006), a fast lane exists. For fuel stations, small sites, and soil-only contamination, simplified procedures (procedura semplificata) allow bonifica to target CSC thresholds directly without a full risk analysis. Those are CEG's Year 1 targets. MOSAICO lets you filter for exactly those.

## Segment 2: mandated cleanup

This is the bigger market, but operationally more complex. When a remediation is court-ordered or ARPA-mandated, CEG's technology needs regulatory approval to be used. That means navigating the Piano di Caratterizzazione, the Analisi di Rischio, and the Piano di Bonifica through ARPA, typically with a qualified geologist or environmental engineer as the responsible technician.

We don't yet have a clear picture of the approval pathway for a foreign technology provider in the Italian context.
