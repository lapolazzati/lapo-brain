# PFAS Remediation via Constructed Wetland (CW) System

*Reference diagram — saved from research*

**File:** [pfas-constructed-wetland-diagram.jpg](pfas-constructed-wetland-diagram.jpg)

## Diagram Overview

The image illustrates a **Constructed Wetland (CW) System** for PFAS remediation, showing four interconnected removal pathways operating in synergy:

### 1. Adsorption (Physical/Chemical)
- Substrate anchors plants and filters PFAS
- Roots modify the microenvironment
- PFAS sequestration into the substrate matrix
- Biofilm formation and immobilization
- Substrate concentrates PFAS for microbes

### 2. Phytoextraction & Rhizosphere
- PFAS uptake by plants and root exudates
- Microbes promote plant growth
- Rhizosphere community supports degradation

### 3. Microbial Degradation
- **Defluorination** (F⁻ release) via defluorase-catalyzed reactions
- **Co-metabolism:** Primary substrate (e.g., glucose) drives microbial activity alongside PFAS
- **Biofilm synergy:** Biofilm community enhances collective degradation
- PFAS transformation and mineralization

### 4. System Integration
- Central Constructed Wetland (CW) System coordinates all pathways
- Arrows indicate cyclic flow: sequestration → uptake → degradation → transformation
- Multi-barrier approach combines physical, chemical, and biological removal

## Relevance to CEG

This diagram represents a **nature-based, passive remediation architecture** for PFAS — distinct from conventional "scavo e smaltimento" (dig-and-haul) approaches dominant in Italy. Key CEG-relevant points:

- **Cost profile:** Lower OPEX than excavation/incineration; suitable for large-area, low-concentration plumes
- **Regulatory angle:** Italian D.Lgs 152/2006 prioritizes risk-based remediation; CW systems can achieve CSC (concentrazioni soglia di contaminazione) targets for soil and groundwater
- **Technology positioning:** CEG's bioremediation approach aligns with constructed wetlands as a scalable, sustainable alternative
- **Market fit:** Veneto PFAS plume (380 km², 300k people exposed) and other large Italian contaminated sites could be candidates for pilot CW deployments
