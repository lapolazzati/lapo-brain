# AETHON - Client Interaction

## One message

Lead client conversations as expansion business cases, not demos, pricing debates, or lead-generation requests.

AETHON's job is to help the client decide whether a new country can become real business, then build the first motion to prove it.

## The three client-interaction principles

### 1. Diagnose before price

Do not answer pricing too early. First understand the workflow, buyer, budget, timing, and alternatives.

### 2. Quantify before proposal

Do not send a proposal until you have enough numbers to build a business case.

Useful numbers:

- Monthly volume.
- Current team effort.
- Time spent manually.
- Cost of current process.
- Cost of delay.
- Number of stakeholders involved.
- Current tools and gaps.
- Budget cycle and approval path.

### 3. Package before discount

Do not discount defensively. Package strategically.

Discount only in exchange for something valuable:

- Early signature.
- Case study.
- Reference.
- Product feedback.
- Integration learning.
- First-country validation.
- Strategic access to a local market.

## How to lead a discovery call

### 1. Set the frame

Open with the business question, not the product.

Script:

> The goal of this call is not to force a demo. The goal is to understand whether this market, use case, or workflow has enough value to justify a proper launch or pilot.

For AETHON clients:

> Before we talk about expansion tactics, I want to understand why this country matters, what you already know, what you have tried, and what would make this market worth a serious investment.

### 2. Map the current process

Ask practical questions.

For software / workflow clients:

- What happens today from lead / candidate / buyer / partner identification to conversion?
- Which parts are manual?
- Which tools are already in place?
- Where does the current tool stop helping?
- How many people touch the process?
- What breaks when volume increases?

For international expansion clients:

- Why this country?
- Why now?
- Who do you believe the buyer is?
- What local alternatives exist?
- What local partners might matter?
- What has to be true for you to invest further?

### 3. Find the economic driver

Every market-entry or product sale needs an economic case.

The economic driver is usually one of these:

- More revenue.
- Lower operating cost.
- Faster time to market.
- Lower expansion risk.
- Better conversion.
- Better compliance.
- Better partner access.
- Lower hiring risk.

Do not assume the driver. Ask and confirm it.

### 4. Identify stakeholders

Map the decision process early.

Questions:

- Who feels the pain most directly?
- Who owns the budget?
- Who signs the contract?
- Who can block the project?
- Who owns implementation?
- Who needs to be in the next call?

Never run a pricing call without knowing who can approve.

### 5. Define success before proposing

Ask:

- What would make this pilot successful?
- What would make this country validation useful?
- What evidence would justify hiring locally?
- What would make you stop the project?
- What decision do you need to make by the end of this work?

## How to handle pricing

Pricing should follow three moves.

### 1. Reframe the price question

When a customer says the product is expensive, do not defend the number immediately.

Reframe the comparison.

Bad frame:

> Our software costs X.

Better frame:

> Let's compare this to the cost of the workflow or market-entry decision we are improving.

Script:

> That is exactly the right question. Before we compare the price to a local salary or a local tool, let's compare the total process cost. If the ROI does not work in local currency, you should not buy it.

### 2. Build the ROI live

Do not calculate the business case after the call if you can build it with the buyer during the call.

Ask:

- How many transactions, roles, candidates, accounts, or opportunities per month?
- How many people touch the process?
- How many hours per week are manual?
- What does that cost the company?
- What happens if the process is delayed?
- What would improvement be worth?

Then say:

> If we can reduce this from X hours to Y hours, or increase conversion from A to B, then the question becomes whether the monthly investment is justified by the operating improvement.

### 3. Anchor the number in units

Annual prices can feel large, especially in local currency. Re-anchor the number into operational units.

Examples:

- Cost per month.
- Cost per recruiter.
- Cost per open role.
- Cost per store.
- Cost per partner conversation.
- Cost per qualified opportunity.
- Cost per market validated.

Bad anchor:

> This costs 60,000 reais per year.

Better anchor:

> This is 5,000 reais per month, or around X per recruiter, Y per open role, and Z percent of the process cost we are replacing.

## Pricing structures

### 1. Standard annual

Use when the customer has budget, urgency, and a clear business case.

Structure:

- 12-month contract.
- Annual or quarterly payment.
- Full implementation.
- Full support.
- Clear success metrics.

### 2. Design partner

Use when the client is strategically valuable but budget or timing is hard.

Structure:

- Discounted first year.
- Client signs now.
- Payment can align with budget timing.
- Implementation starts early.
- Client commits to feedback, reference value, or case study if successful.

Script:

> We can create special first-year terms if this becomes a real design partnership. That means we start early, learn the local workflow together, and in exchange for better terms we receive structured feedback, a reference path, and a clear implementation commitment.

### 3. Paid POC

Use when value is plausible but not yet proven.

Structure:

- One workflow.
- One business unit, brand, store, country segment, or partner path.
- 60 to 90 days.
- Clear KPI.
- POC fee credited into annual contract if converted.

Script:

> Let's not start with the whole company. Let's pick one workflow, measure the value, and decide whether it deserves a broader rollout.

Avoid free pilots unless there is a very strong strategic reason.

## Discount rules

Never discount because the buyer asked.

Discount only when you get at least one of the following:

1. Speed: signature now or implementation now.
2. Proof: case study, reference, or testimonial.
3. Learning: strategic market, integration, or local workflow insight.

Suggested language:

> The standard price is X. For a first-country design partner, we can offer Y, but only if we agree on the learning agenda, implementation timeline, and reference path.

## Common objections and responses

### Objection 1: We have local players

Response:

> That makes sense. The question is not whether local tools exist. The question is which part of the workflow they solve, which part remains manual, and whether there is a gap worth solving.

Then ask:

- What do local players do well?
- Where do they stop?
- What still happens manually?
- What would you change if you could?

### Objection 2: This is expensive in local currency

Response:

> I agree that a simple currency conversion can make the number look wrong. Let's build the local ROI instead. If the savings, revenue, or risk reduction do not justify the price in local currency, we should not move forward.

Then build the ROI live.

### Objection 3: We just implemented another tool

Response:

> That may actually be good. We do not need to replace what is already working. Let's identify what the new tool covers and what still sits outside it.

Then ask:

- What does the tool solve?
- What does it not solve?
- Where are people still using spreadsheets, WhatsApp, email, calls, or manual coordination?
- Is there an integration or workflow layer that would create value?

### Objection 4: We have no budget until later

Response:

> Understood. Is the budget cycle a hard blocker, or is there a smaller design partner or pilot structure that could be signed now and paid later, so implementation can start before the next budget cycle?

Then propose:

- Sign now, pay later.
- Paid POC.
- Design partner first-year terms.
- Budget-aligned payment schedule.

### Objection 5: Can you do the cold calls?

Response:

> I can lead the first calibration calls and senior conversations, but high-volume calling should be handled by a trained local operator. My role is to design the motion, validate the market, manage quality, and step into strategic conversations.

Then propose a pod:

- AETHON: strategy and quality.
- Local BD: volume outreach and follow-up.
- Client: product expertise, demo, closing, and implementation.

## Klaaryo pricing lesson

The key lesson from the Klaaryo Brazil conversation is that pricing cannot be framed as European software converted into reais.

The better frame is:

> What process cost are we replacing or reducing?

Important lessons:

1. Do not compare software price only to local salary.
2. Build ROI around the workflow: hours saved, process speed, candidate experience, open-day logistics, integrations, and recruiter productivity.
3. Use local pricing anchors: monthly cost, cost per recruiter, cost per open role, or cost per store.

The customer's concern was not only price. It was whether the product would make sense against local alternatives, local salary levels, budget timing, and recently implemented tools.

## Next-call structure after a pricing objection

Use this agenda.

### 1. Confirm the real pain

Script:

> From the last conversation, I understood that the key issue may not be overall turnover. It may be the manual coordination around specific recruiting workflows. Is that correct?

### 2. Map one workflow

Pick one use case and map it end to end.

Example:

- Open-day invitations.
- Candidate screening.
- WhatsApp follow-up.
- Scheduling.
- No-show reduction.
- Store-manager coordination.
- ATS / HCM handoff.

### 3. Quantify the cost

Ask:

- How many candidates?
- How many roles?
- How many people involved?
- How many hours?
- What is manual?
- What does delay cost?

### 4. Define success

Ask:

- What would make this worth doing?
- What KPI matters most?
- What would justify expansion?

### 5. Propose the commercial path

Offer only after discovery.

Potential paths:

- Annual contract.
- Design partner.
- Paid POC.

### 6. Bring the right stakeholders

End with:

> If this makes sense, the next conversation should include the budget owner, the workflow owner, and the implementation owner.

## Partner identification

Partner identification is part of country launch, not an afterthought.

The purpose is to answer:

> Who can help us enter this market faster, more credibly, or with less risk?

## The three partner buckets

### 1. Commercial access partners

These partners help reach buyers.

Examples:

- Resellers.
- Distributors.
- Channel partners.
- Industry associations.
- Strategic accounts.
- Local sales partners.
- VC / PE portfolio introductions.

Use when:

- Direct access is hard.
- Trust is local.
- The buyer needs a local reference.
- Market entry depends on relationships.

### 2. Infrastructure and integration partners

These partners make the product or service work locally.

Examples:

- ATS / HCM platforms.
- Payment processors.
- Sponsor banks.
- Labs.
- Compliance vendors.
- Implementation partners.
- Data providers.
- Local technology platforms.

Use when:

- The product must integrate with local systems.
- The market has local technical standards.
- Buyers already depend on a dominant platform.
- Partner integration can unlock distribution.

### 3. Credibility and governance partners

These partners reduce trust, regulatory, or institutional risk.

Examples:

- Law firms.
- Tax advisors.
- Regulatory advisors.
- Local board advisors.
- Sector experts.
- Procurement advisors.
- Academic or institutional partners.

Use when:

- Regulation is unclear.
- Procurement is complex.
- The client needs local credibility.
- Senior buyers need reassurance.

## Partner scorecard

Score each potential partner from 1 to 5.

### 1. Access

Do they reach the buyer or stakeholder we need?

### 2. Credibility

Will their name increase trust locally?

### 3. Strategic fit

Do they benefit if the client succeeds in the market?

### 4. Execution ability

Can they actually do the work, or are they only a logo?

### 5. Speed

Can they move within the timeline of the validation sprint?

### 6. Economic logic

Is there a clear business model for both sides?

### 7. Risk

Could they slow the process, create dependency, or misrepresent the client?

Prioritize partners with high access, high credibility, and high speed.

## Partner mapping process

### 1. Build the universe

Create a long list of possible partners from public data, investor networks, industry associations, client knowledge, and local experts.

### 2. Segment the universe

Classify partners by type:

- Commercial access.
- Infrastructure / integration.
- Credibility / governance.

### 3. Prioritize

Use the partner scorecard.

### 4. Create the partnership thesis

For each priority partner, write:

- Why this partner matters.
- What they gain.
- What the client gains.
- What the first conversation should test.
- What the next step could be.

### 5. Run discovery conversations

Do not pitch too early. First test appetite.

Questions:

- Do you see demand for this locally?
- Who would buy it?
- What objections would appear?
- What local requirement are we missing?
- Would you partner, refer, integrate, or advise?
- What would make this commercially attractive for you?

### 6. Formalize only after signal

Do not waste time on partnership agreements before there is clear signal.

First validate:

- Interest.
- Fit.
- Economics.
- Speed.
- Trust.

Then define:

- Referral agreement.
- Reseller model.
- Implementation partnership.
- Integration path.
- Joint pilot.
- Advisory agreement.

## VC and PE introduction strategy

VCs and PEs are not only client sources. They are partner channels.

### What they care about

- Helping portfolio companies grow.
- International expansion without wasted capital.
- Faster go / no-go decisions.
- More useful platform support.
- Lower hiring risk.

### What to offer

Portfolio Expansion Radar.

AETHON scans the portfolio and identifies:

- Which companies are expansion-ready.
- Which target countries make sense.
- What trigger signals exist.
- What blockers are likely.
- What first action to take.

### Intro ask

Use a narrow ask.

Script:

> I am building an AI-enabled country launch studio. I help companies test new countries before they hire locally. For your portfolio, I can identify the 3 to 5 companies most likely to benefit from Brazil, Italy, the US, or Europe expansion and produce a practical first-step memo. Would it be useful to run this on a small sample?

## Client handoff to country manager

Finding the country manager should not end the AETHON relationship.

The handoff should have three stages.

### 1. Hiring profile

AETHON defines the role based on market learnings.

Outputs:

- Scorecard.
- Job description.
- Required experience.
- Interview questions.
- Compensation logic.
- Candidate-source map.

### 2. Ramp plan

AETHON helps the new country manager start fast.

Outputs:

- First 30 / 60 / 90 days.
- Priority accounts.
- Priority partners.
- CRM context.
- Objection library.
- Local GTM playbook.

### 3. Expansion board

AETHON stays involved as advisor and quality control.

Outputs:

- Monthly pipeline review.
- Partner review.
- Market intelligence update.
- Coaching for local lead.
- Strategic escalation.

Positioning:

> We do not disappear when the country manager is hired. We help install, ramp, and govern the local motion until it is stable.

### 2. Market signal log

## When to proceed, pause, or stop

### Proceed when

- The pain is clear.
- The buyer has urgency.
- The market has reachable accounts or partners.
- There is budget or a strategic reason to test.
- The client is willing to adapt locally.

### Pause when

- Budget timing is real but future interest is credible.
- The right stakeholder is not yet involved.
- More partner validation is needed.
- The local workflow is unclear.

### Stop when

- The client cannot sell in its home market.
- The buyer wants only cheap labor.
- The economics do not work.
- The client refuses to localize.
- No one can define success.
- The market signal is consistently weak.

## The main interaction rule

> Never let the client reduce AETHON to cold calls, translations, or a price quote.

Your role is to lead the expansion decision.

The conversation should always move through three steps:

1. What market question are we answering?
2. What evidence will prove it?
3. What operating path should we take next?
